  • In 1962, a scientist from MIT and ARPA named J.C.R. Licklider proposed a network of computers that could talk to one another.
  • In 1965, another MIT scientist developed a way of sending information from one computer to another by using packet switching. Packet switching breaks data down information into blocks, or packets, before sending it to its destination. That way, each packet can take its own route from place to place.
  • On October 29, 1969, ARPAnet delivered its first message between two computers in different locations.
  • By the end of the 1970s, a computer scientist named Vinton Cerf created Transmission Control Protocol or TCP which served as a handshake that introduces different, distant computers to each other.
  • One of the most transformative world-changing inventions in the history of humanity is the world wide web. By the early 1990s, the web was basically just an obscure research network invented a few years earlier by an English scientist called Tim Berners-Lee.

  • University of Illinois computer science student Marc Andreessen imagined a future where everyone including regular people would use the web as part of our daily lives. Andreessen soon had a bunch of his college programming pals working on making the web easier and more intuitive to use by adding images, pictures, audio and video.
